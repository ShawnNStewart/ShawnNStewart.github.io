# Can you interpret the results?

Interpreting the results - from the table, we can see that for true “down” values, the model predicted correctly for 35 results and incorrectly for 76 results, giving it a 68% error rate. For true “up” values, it predicted correctly for 106 results and incorrectly for 35 results, giving it a 25% error rate. If we assume that stock brokers are more concerned about losing money when the market goes down, we might infer that they would be more concerned about errors that overestimate the likelihood of the stock market going up. In that case, it may make sense to change the threshold to reduce the errors for true “down” values.

Questions

What is/are the requirement(s) of LDA?

With LDA, the distribution of predictors should be approximately normal. Specifically for LDA, we assume that \(f_k(x)\) is normal or Gaussian.

How LDA is different from Logistic Regression?

Logistic regression takes only binary dependent variables, while LDA can be extended to multiple categories (note - multinomial logistic regression can also work with multiple categories). LDA is also more stable than logistic regression when there is substantial separation between the categories, and is more accurate than logistic regression if the distribution of predictors is mostly normal and the sample size is small.

What is ROC?

ROC is an acronym for “receiver operating characteristics.” The ROC summarizes the overall performance of a classifier over all possible thresholds (ISLR pg 151). We can use ROC curves to compare different classifiers.

What is sensitivity and specificity? Which is more important in your opinion?

Sensitivity and specificity can represent how well a classifier performs (ISLR pg 149). Sensitivity reflects the number of true positive cases identified positively and specificity reflects the number of true negative cases identified positively. Both are important, and which is more relevant will depend on the details of the analysis. In the ISLR example, the classifier was set to classify which individuals will default on a loan (defaulters), so the sensitivity related to the positive case of defaulters who were correctly identified. However, if the binary categories were flipped so that the classifier is defining non-defaulters, then the sensitivity would refer to non-defaulters.


Whether we care more about false negatives or false positives depends on the specifics of what we’re modeling. In some cases, we may want to minimize false negatives, when the consequences of a false positive are more severe. In other cases, we may want to minimize false positives. For instance, if we are evaluating COVID tests, the consequences of a false positive (think you have COVID but you don’t) may be less severe than the consequences of a false negative (think you’re clear, but you have COVID). In the first instance, people may need to take extra precautions and their schedule may disrupted. In the second instance, people may unknowingly spread disease.
